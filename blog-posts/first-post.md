Composition using Python and Abjad/LilyPond: Life Beyond Notation Software is a proof of concept for something I have wanted to do for a long time: turn composition ideas I had decades ago into notated scores that can also be heard. After many years of intense academic work, including my current role as a computer science department chair, I returned to formal music study at Loyola University Chicago and started looking for a way to compose that felt closer to how I already think and work. This project is my first serious attempt to do that in public. It is also a way of testing whether software practice and composition practice can meet on equal terms.

The project brings together finished pieces, small compositional studies, and exploratory systems built with [Python](https://www.python.org/), [Abjad](https://abjad.github.io/), and [LilyPond](https://lilypond.org/), with audio rendering through [FluidSynth](https://www.fluidsynth.org/), [FFmpeg](https://ffmpeg.org/), and carefully chosen SoundFonts. These open source tools made it possible for me to move from code to engraved score to MIDI and WAV in a way that is reproducible, inspectable, and easy to share. That matters because one of the goals here is not only to make music, but to document a process for making music in a clear and technically responsible way. The code, builds, releases, and documentation are all part of the project.

Readers might care about this work for several reasons. It may be of interest to composers who are curious about plain-text workflows, to people in music technology who want a concrete example of code-driven score generation, and to anyone interested in what open source tools can make possible outside their usual domains. For me, it is also personal. These are ideas I had a long time ago, but I did not have the right environment, discipline, or tools to bring them into notated and audible form. This repository shows that I can now begin doing that, even if the work is still early and much remains to be improved musically and technically.

The best place to explore the project is the documentation site at [compositions.gkt.sh](https://compositions.gkt.sh), where you can browse the current works, read the technical report, download scores, and listen to the available renderings. The source repository is on [GitHub](https://github.com/gkthiruvathukal/compositions-abjad), and the report is archived on [Figshare](https://figshare.com/articles/online_resource/Composition_using_Python_and_Abjad_LilyPond_Life_Beyond_Notation_Software/31827391) with DOI [10.6084/m9.figshare.31827391.v1](https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.31827391.v1). This is not the finished destination. It is the beginning of a larger body of work, and I think it is a very good start.
